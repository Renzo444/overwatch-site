The Gentlemen ransomware: Dissecting a self-propagating Go encryptor

By Overwatch Observatory — Automated Detection Engineering

Threat Overview

Microsoft Security recently published a detailed analysis of "The Gentlemen ransomware," a self-propagating Go encryptor. This report provided extensive behavioral indicators, including specific command-line arguments and targeted processes, which are invaluable for developing high-fidelity detections against this threat. Our analysis focused on translating these insights into actionable KQL queries for defenders.

We developed several KQL queries targeting the `DeviceProcessEvents` table in Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, focusing on the distinct behaviors outlined in the report. For persistence (T1053.005), we look for `schtasks.exe` creating a scheduled task named "gentlemen_system" with SYSTEM privileges, a clear indicator of the ransomware establishing a foothold.

Our queries also address defense evasion and system impact. We detect T1562.001 by monitoring `powershell.exe` or `pwsh.exe` executing `Set-MpPreference` to disable real-time monitoring or add exclusions, directly impairing endpoint security. Additionally, we identify T1070.001 when `wevtutil.exe` is used to clear critical System, Application, or Security event logs, an attempt to remove forensic evidence.

For impact, we generated queries for T1490 and T1489. We detect attempts to inhibit system recovery by looking for `vssadmin.exe` or `wmic.exe` deleting shadow copies. For service stop, our query identifies `taskkill.exe` forcefully terminating multiple processes from a specific list of targeted applications and services, such as `vmms` or `sqlservr`, as detailed in the Microsoft Security report. This aggregation helps surface malicious activity while minimizing false positives.

Defenders should implement these KQL queries in their environments to proactively detect "The Gentlemen ransomware" and similar threats. We recommend reviewing alerts generated by these patterns, tuning them as necessary, and regularly auditing for the specific command-line arguments and process behaviors we have outlined.

Detection Rules

Privilege Escalation / T1053.005
KQL 
DeviceProcessEvents
| where ProcessVersionInfoOriginalFileName =~ "schtasks.exe"
| where ProcessCommandLine has_all ("create", "/tn", "gentlemen_system", "/ru", "SYSTEM")
Defense Evasion / T1562.001
KQL 
DeviceProcessEvents
| where ProcessVersionInfoOriginalFileName in~ ("powershell.exe", "pwsh.exe")
| where ProcessCommandLine has "Set-MpPreference"
| where ProcessCommandLine has_any (
    "-DisableRealtimeMonitoring $true",
    "-ExclusionPath C:\"
)
Impact / T1490
KQL 
DeviceProcessEvents
| where (ProcessVersionInfoOriginalFileName =~ "vssadmin.exe" and ProcessCommandLine has_all ("delete", "shadows", "/all"))
    or (ProcessVersionInfoOriginalFileName =~ "wmic.exe" and ProcessCommandLine has_all ("shadowcopy", "delete"))
Impact / T1489
KQL 
let targeted_processes = dynamic(["vmms", "vmwp", "sqlservr", "sqlbrowser", "SQLAGENT", "sqlwriter", "mysqld", "postgres", "oracle", "VeeamNFSSvc", "bedbh", "vxmon", "cbService", "saphostexec", "excel", "winword", "powerpnt", "outlook", "TeamViewer_Service", "QBIDPService"]);
DeviceProcessEvents
| where ProcessVersionInfoOriginalFileName =~ "taskkill.exe"
| where ProcessCommandLine has "/f" and ProcessCommandLine has "/im"
| parse ProcessCommandLine with * "/im " TargetProcessToKill
| where TargetProcessToKill has_any (targeted_processes)
| summarize KilledProcesses=make_set(TargetProcessToKill), KillCount=dcount(TargetProcessToKill) by InitiatingProcessId, bin(TimeGenerated, 5m), DeviceName, AccountName
| where KillCount > 5
Defense Evasion / T1070.001
KQL 
DeviceProcessEvents
| where ProcessVersionInfoOriginalFileName =~ "wevtutil.exe"
| where ProcessCommandLine has_any ("cl", "clear-log") and ProcessCommandLine has_any ("System", "Application", "Security")
